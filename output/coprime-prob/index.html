Relatively Prime, Relatively Often

Say you choose two integers. What’s the chance that they’re coprime? My intuition says that it can’t be more than one-half, because half of all numbers share a factor of 2. But I’m thoroughly wrong here. The probability is actually \(\frac{6}{\pi^2}\), which is about \(61\%\).

We wish to find the probability that two randomly selected integers \(m\) and \(n\) are coprime. If this is true, then \(\mathrm{gcd}(m,n) = 1\). Let’s say that \(p = \mathrm{P}(\mathrm{gcd}(m,n)=1)\).

Consider \(p_k = \mathrm{P}(\mathrm{gcd}(a,b)=k)\) for some positive integer \(k\). For this to be true, \(k\) must divide both \(a\) and \(b\), and \(\frac{a}{k}\) and \(\frac{b}{k}\) must be coprime. And so \(p_k = \mathrm{P}(k|a\,\text{and}\,k|b)\cdot\mathrm{P}\left(\mathrm{gcd}\left(\frac{a}{k},\frac{b}{k}\right)\right)\). One in every \(k\) integers is divisible by \(k\). So the first probability, namely that two random integers are both divisible by \(k\), is \(\frac{1}{k^2}\). The second probability is just \(p\)—because \(a\) and \(b\) were random, so are \(\frac{a}{k}\) and \(\frac{b}{k}\). And so \(p_k = \frac{p}{k^2}\)

Two numbers must have some greatest common divisor. And so the sum of \(p_k\) over all \(k\) must be 1. That is, \[\begin{equation} \begin{split} \sum\limits_{k=1}^\infty \frac{p}{k^2} &= 1 \\ p &= \frac{1}{\sum\limits_{k=1}^\infty \frac{1}{k^2}}. \end{split} \end{equation}\] The denominator is famously equal to \(\frac{\pi^2}{6}\), as shown by Euler. And so we conclude that \(p = \frac{6}{\pi^2}\).
